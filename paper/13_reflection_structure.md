# Reflection symmetry and the finite Weil operator system

## 1. Scope

This chapter isolates the exact finite-dimensional structure forced by a reflection-symmetric Gaussian packet family. It distinguishes three objects that should not be conflated:

1. the packet Hilbert space with its Gram geometry;
2. the real self-adjoint operator system generated by the explicit-formula kernels;
3. the unital star-algebra generated by those kernels under multiplication.

The central exact result is that reflection decomposes the finite generalized eigenproblem into even and odd sectors. For the completed-zeta pole matrix, the update is positive rank one on the even sector and negative rank one on the odd sector.

This chapter does not claim that the generated algebra is always maximal inside the reflection commutant. That is posed as a finite computational and algebraic question.

## 2. Packet Hilbert space

Let

\[
g_j(x)=\exp\left(-\frac{(x-c_j)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right),
\qquad j=1,\dots,N,
\]

with pairwise distinct centers and common width \(\sigma>0\). Let

\[
\mathcal H_N=\mathbb C^N
\]

be the coefficient space, where \(v\in\mathbb C^N\) represents

\[
f_v(x)=\sum_{j=1}^N v_jg_j(x).
\]

The intrinsic inner product is

\[
\langle u,v\rangle_B=u^*Bv,
\]

where

\[
B_{ij}
=
\sqrt\pi\,\sigma
\exp\left(-\frac{(c_i-c_j)^2}{4\sigma^2}\right).
\]

For distinct centers the Gaussian Gram matrix is positive definite. Thus the generalized Hermitian problem

\[
Av=\lambda Bv
\]

is equivalent to the ordinary Hermitian problem

\[
B^{-1/2}AB^{-1/2}y=\lambda y.
\]

## 3. Reflection operator

Assume the center set is invariant under reflection:

\[
\{c_1,\dots,c_N\}=\{-c_1,\dots,-c_N\}.
\]

Because the centers are distinct, there is a unique involutive permutation \(\pi\) satisfying

\[
c_{\pi(i)}=-c_i.
\]

Define the permutation matrix \(J\) by

\[
Je_i=e_{\pi(i)}.
\]

Then

\[
J^*=J,
\qquad
J^2=I.
\]

Its eigenspaces are

\[
\mathcal H_N^+=\ker(J-I),
\qquad
\mathcal H_N^-=\ker(J+I).
\]

They will be called the even and odd sectors.

## 4. Even difference-kernel matrices commute with reflection

Let \(\phi:\mathbb R\to\mathbb C\) be even and define

\[
K_\phi(i,j)=\phi(c_i-c_j).
\]

Then

\[
K_\phi(\pi(i),\pi(j))
=
\phi(c_{\pi(i)}-c_{\pi(j)})
=
\phi(-(c_i-c_j))
=
\phi(c_i-c_j).
\]

Hence

\[
J^*K_\phi J=K_\phi,
\]

or equivalently

\[
JK_\phi=K_\phi J.
\]

This one lemma contains the reflection argument for every current finite block.

## 5. Application to the Gram, conductor, gamma, and prime blocks

### 5.1 Gram and conductor

The Gram kernel

\[
\phi_B(x)=\sqrt\pi\,\sigma e^{-x^2/(4\sigma^2)}
\]

is even, so

\[
JB=BJ.
\]

The conductor block is a real scalar multiple of \(B\), hence also commutes with \(J\).

### 5.2 Gamma block

The gamma kernel has the form

\[
\phi_\Gamma(x)
=
2\sigma^2\int_0^\infty
\omega_a(t)e^{-(\sigma t)^2}\cos(tx)\,dt.
\]

Since cosine is even,

\[
\phi_\Gamma(-x)=\phi_\Gamma(x),
\]

and therefore

\[
JA_\Gamma=A_\Gamma J.
\]

### 5.3 Paired prime translations

For a shift \(s>0\), define

\[
\phi_s(x)
=
-\sqrt\pi\,\sigma
\left[
\exp\left(-\frac{(x-s)^2}{4\sigma^2}\right)
+
\exp\left(-\frac{(x+s)^2}{4\sigma^2}\right)
\right].
\]

The two summands exchange under \(x\mapsto-x\), so \(\phi_s\) is even. Thus every paired translation matrix \(T_s\) commutes with \(J\). Any finite real linear combination, including the weighted prime-power block, also commutes with \(J\):

\[
JA_{\mathrm{prime}}=A_{\mathrm{prime}}J.
\]

Consequently, for the no-pole finite Weil matrix

\[
A=A_{\mathrm{cond}}+A_\Gamma+A_{\mathrm{prime}},
\]

we have

\[
JA=AJ,
\qquad
JB=BJ.
\]

## 6. Exact parity decomposition of the generalized problem

Because \(A\) and \(B\) commute with \(J\), both sectors are invariant. In a parity-adapted basis,

\[
A=
\begin{pmatrix}
A_+&0\\
0&A_-
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
B=
\begin{pmatrix}
B_+&0\\
0&B_-
\end{pmatrix}.
\]

Therefore

\[
Av=\lambda Bv
\]

splits into

\[
A_+v_+=\lambda B_+v_+,
\qquad
A_-v_-=\lambda B_-v_-.
\]

Since \(B^{-1/2}\) is a spectral function of \(B\), it also commutes with \(J\). Exact Gram whitening therefore preserves the decomposition.

## 7. Pole matrix and parity signature

For the completed zeta function, define

\[
(p_+)_j
=
\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}\,e^{\sigma^2/8}e^{c_j/2},
\qquad
(p_-)_j
=
\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}\,e^{\sigma^2/8}e^{-c_j/2}.
\]

Reflection exchanges these vectors:

\[
Jp_+=p_-,
\qquad
Jp_-=p_+.
\]

Define

\[
p_e=\frac{p_++p_-}{\sqrt2},
\qquad
p_o=\frac{p_+-p_-}{\sqrt2}.
\]

Then

\[
Jp_e=p_e,
\qquad
Jp_o=-p_o,
\]

so \(p_e\in\mathcal H_N^+\) and \(p_o\in\mathcal H_N^-\).

The pole matrix

\[
P=p_-p_+^*+p_+p_-^*
\]

has the exact decomposition

\[
\boxed{P=p_ep_e^*-p_op_o^*.}
\]

Therefore

\[
P|_{\mathcal H_N^+}=p_ep_e^*\succeq0,
\]

and

\[
P|_{\mathcal H_N^-}=-p_op_o^*\preceq0.
\]

Each restriction has rank at most one.

## 8. Sector monotonicity

For nonzero \(v\in\mathcal H_N^+\),

\[
\frac{v^*(A+P)v}{v^*Bv}
=
\frac{v^*Av}{v^*Bv}
+
\frac{|p_e^*v|^2}{v^*Bv}
\ge
\frac{v^*Av}{v^*Bv}.
\]

The Courant-Fischer principle gives

\[
\lambda_k^+(A+P,B)
\ge
\lambda_k^+(A,B).
\]

For nonzero \(v\in\mathcal H_N^-\),

\[
\frac{v^*(A+P)v}{v^*Bv}
=
\frac{v^*Av}{v^*Bv}
-
\frac{|p_o^*v|^2}{v^*Bv}
\le
\frac{v^*Av}{v^*Bv},
\]

and therefore

\[
\lambda_k^-(A+P,B)
\le
\lambda_k^-(A,B).
\]

Thus every even generalized eigenvalue weakly increases and every odd generalized eigenvalue weakly decreases.

Rank-one interlacing further implies that at most one eigenvalue in each sector can cross any fixed level as the pole is added.

## 9. Operator system versus generated algebra

Define the real linear operator system at a finite set of shifts \(S\subset(0,\infty)\) by

\[
\mathscr S_N(S)
=
\operatorname{span}_{\mathbb R}
\{B,\Gamma_0,\Gamma_1,P,T_s:s\in S\}.
\]

Every element is real symmetric and commutes with \(J\). Arithmetic data select particular Hermitian elements of this universal geometric space through the coefficients of the conductor, gamma, prime-power, and pole terms.

The unital star-algebra generated under multiplication is

\[
\mathfrak W_N(S)
=
\operatorname{alg}^*
\bigl(B,\Gamma_0,\Gamma_1,P,J,T_s:s\in S\bigr).
\]

Since every generator commutes with \(J\),

\[
\mathfrak W_N(S)\subseteq\operatorname{Comm}(J).
\]

In a parity-adapted basis,

\[
\operatorname{Comm}(J)
\cong
M_{N_+}(\mathbb C)\oplus M_{N_-}(\mathbb C).
\]

The central finite structural question is whether the inclusion is an equality.

## 10. The common commutant test

Define

\[
\mathfrak W_N(S)'
=
\{X:[X,G]=0\text{ for every generator }G\}.
\]

At minimum,

\[
I,J\in\mathfrak W_N(S)'.
\]

Therefore

\[
\dim\mathfrak W_N(S)'\ge2.
\]

The first diagnostic is

\[
\boxed{\dim\mathfrak W_N(S)'\stackrel{?}=2.}
\]

If the common commutant is exactly \(\operatorname{span}\{I,J\}\), then parity is the complete common symmetry of the chosen generators. Over the complex numbers, finite-dimensional double-commutant and irreducibility results then identify the generated algebra with the full parity-preserving block algebra.

If the dimension is larger than two, every additional common-commutant direction indicates either an extra exact symmetry or a degeneracy caused by the chosen finite geometry or generator set.

## 11. Finite boundary noncommutativity

On an equally spaced symmetric center grid, every current kernel matrix is symmetric Toeplitz. The linear space of symmetric Toeplitz matrices is not generally closed under multiplication. Thus universal difference kernels can generate a noncommutative algebra after finite compression, even though ideal translation-invariant convolution operators commute in the infinite setting.

For two finite kernel matrices define the boundary commutator defect

\[
D_N(K,L)=[K,L]
\]

and its Gram-whitened magnitude

\[
\delta_N(K,L)
=
\left\|
B^{-1/2}[K,L]B^{-1/2}
\right\|.
\]

This quantity measures failure of the finite packet section to preserve commutative convolution structure. Its behavior under interval expansion, grid refinement, and width scaling is an open computational problem.

## 12. Pole susceptibility

After parity reduction and whitening, write the odd block as

\[
C_-=B_-^{-1/2}A_-B_-^{-1/2}
\]

and

\[
u_o=B_-^{-1/2}p_o.
\]

If \(C_->0\), then the odd updated block

\[
C_--u_ou_o^*
\]

is positive definite precisely when

\[
\eta_o:=u_o^*C_-^{-1}u_o<1.
\]

The scalar \(\eta_o\) is a natural odd pole-susceptibility invariant. Equality gives a zero mode, while \(\eta_o>1\) produces exactly one negative odd direction.

Analogous resolvent quantities can be defined in the even sector, with additional care when the no-pole even block is indefinite.

## 13. Immediate computational program

The next finite experiments should:

1. construct the exact reflection matrix and parity-adapted bases;
2. verify commutator residuals with \(J\) for every generator;
3. compute the numerical dimension of the common commutant;
4. test whether selected generators already generate the full parity-preserving algebra;
5. measure Gram-whitened pairwise commutator defects under geometric refinement;
6. compute even and odd pole susceptibilities;
7. track all quantities as packet width, spacing, interval size, prime cutoff, and prime smoothing change.

These experiments should distinguish theorem-level identities from tolerance-dependent numerical conclusions.

## 14. Claim status

The reflection commutation, parity splitting, pole signature, and sector monotonicity statements above are exact finite-dimensional consequences of the stated Gaussian packet definitions and paired explicit-formula kernels.

The maximality of the generated algebra, the asymptotic behavior of the boundary commutator defect, and any universality of the pole susceptibilities remain open questions.
