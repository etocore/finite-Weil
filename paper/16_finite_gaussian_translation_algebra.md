# Chapter 16. The Structure of the Finite Gaussian Translation Algebra

## 16.1 Motivation

The preceding chapters established two fundamental facts about the paired Gaussian translation operators on an equally spaced symmetric packet grid.

First, for any interval \(I\subset \mathbb R\) with nonempty interior,

\[
\operatorname{span}\{T_s:s\in I\}
=
\mathcal T_N^{\mathrm{sym}},
\]

the full space of real symmetric Toeplitz matrices.

Second,

\[
\bigl(\mathcal T_N^{\mathrm{sym}}\bigr)'
=
\operatorname{span}_{\mathbb C}\{I,J\},
\]

where \(J\) is the reflection operator

\[
Je_i=e_{N+1-i}.
\]

These two statements completely determine the unital \(*\)-algebra generated by the Gaussian translations. The purpose of this chapter is to identify that algebra explicitly and record the resulting parity decomposition, center, and irreducibility properties.

---

## 16.2 Definition

Let

\[
\mathcal H_N=\mathbb C^N.
\]

For the equally spaced symmetric packet grid, define the finite Gaussian translation algebra by

\[
\boxed{
\mathfrak G_N(I)
=
\operatorname{alg}^{*}\{T_s:s\in I\},
}
\]

where \(I\subset \mathbb R\) has nonempty interior and \(\operatorname{alg}^{*}\) denotes the smallest unital \(*\)-subalgebra of \(M_N(\mathbb C)\) containing the indicated family.

Each \(T_s\) is real symmetric, hence self-adjoint:

\[
T_s^*=T_s.
\]

The algebra is therefore finite-dimensional and closed under adjoints.

Because the span theorem holds on every interval with nonempty interior, the resulting algebra is independent of the particular interval. We may therefore write simply

\[
\mathfrak G_N
=
\mathfrak G_N(I).
\]

---

## 16.3 Main Structure Theorem

### Theorem 16.1 (Finite Gaussian Translation Algebra)

Assume the packet centers are equally spaced and symmetric about the origin. Then

\[
\boxed{
\mathfrak G_N
=
\{J\}'
}
\]

and therefore

\[
\boxed{
\mathfrak G_N
\cong
M_{\lceil N/2\rceil}(\mathbb C)
\oplus
M_{\lfloor N/2\rfloor}(\mathbb C).
}
\]

Equivalently, the finite Gaussian translation algebra consists precisely of all operators preserving reflection parity.

Moreover,

\[
\boxed{
Z(\mathfrak G_N)
=
\operatorname{span}_{\mathbb C}\{I,J\}.
}
\]

Thus reflection is the unique nontrivial central symmetry of the algebra.

### Proof

From the Gaussian span theorem,

\[
\operatorname{span}\{T_s:s\in I\}
=
\mathcal T_N^{\mathrm{sym}}.
\]

Hence

\[
\mathfrak G_N
=
\operatorname{alg}^{*}(\mathcal T_N^{\mathrm{sym}}).
\]

The Toeplitz commutant theorem gives

\[
(\mathcal T_N^{\mathrm{sym}})'
=
\operatorname{span}_{\mathbb C}\{I,J\}.
\]

Since \(\mathfrak G_N\) is a unital finite-dimensional \(*\)-subalgebra of \(M_N(\mathbb C)\), the finite-dimensional bicommutant theorem gives

\[
\mathfrak G_N
=
\mathfrak G_N''.
\]

Because taking commutants depends only on linear span,

\[
\mathfrak G_N'
=
(\mathcal T_N^{\mathrm{sym}})'
=
\operatorname{span}_{\mathbb C}\{I,J\}.
\]

Therefore

\[
\mathfrak G_N
=
\bigl(\operatorname{span}_{\mathbb C}\{I,J\}\bigr)'
=
\{J\}'.
\]

Now decompose

\[
\mathcal H_N
=
\mathcal H_+\oplus\mathcal H_-,
\]

where

\[
\mathcal H_+
=
\ker(J-I),
\qquad
\mathcal H_-
=
\ker(J+I).
\]

The dimensions are

\[
\dim \mathcal H_+
=
\left\lceil\frac N2\right\rceil,
\qquad
\dim \mathcal H_-
=
\left\lfloor\frac N2\right\rfloor.
\]

An operator commutes with \(J\) if and only if it preserves this decomposition. Relative to \(\mathcal H_+\oplus\mathcal H_-\), every element of \(\{J\}'\) has the form

\[
\begin{pmatrix}
A_+&0\\
0&A_-
\end{pmatrix},
\]

with

\[
A_+\in M_{\lceil N/2\rceil}(\mathbb C),
\qquad
A_-\in M_{\lfloor N/2\rfloor}(\mathbb C).
\]

Conversely, every such block-diagonal operator commutes with \(J\). Hence

\[
\mathfrak G_N
\cong
M_{\lceil N/2\rceil}(\mathbb C)
\oplus
M_{\lfloor N/2\rfloor}(\mathbb C).
\]

Finally, the center of a direct sum of two full matrix algebras consists of scalar operators on each block:

\[
Z(\mathfrak G_N)
=
\{a\Pi_++b\Pi_-:a,b\in\mathbb C\},
\]

where

\[
\Pi_+=\frac{I+J}{2},
\qquad
\Pi_-=\frac{I-J}{2}.
\]

Equivalently,

\[
Z(\mathfrak G_N)
=
\operatorname{span}_{\mathbb C}\{I,J\}.
\]

\(\square\)

---

## 16.4 Parity as the Complete Central Decomposition

The projections

\[
\Pi_+=\frac{I+J}{2},
\qquad
\Pi_-=\frac{I-J}{2}
\]

are the two minimal central projections of \(\mathfrak G_N\). They satisfy

\[
\Pi_+\Pi_-=0,
\qquad
\Pi_++\Pi_-=I,
\]

and decompose the algebra as

\[
\mathfrak G_N
=
\Pi_+\mathfrak G_N\Pi_+
\oplus
\Pi_-\mathfrak G_N\Pi_-.
\]

Each summand is a full matrix algebra:

\[
\Pi_+\mathfrak G_N\Pi_+
\cong
M_{\lceil N/2\rceil}(\mathbb C),
\]

\[
\Pi_-\mathfrak G_N\Pi_-
\cong
M_{\lfloor N/2\rfloor}(\mathbb C).
\]

Thus parity is not merely a convenient block decomposition. It is the complete central decomposition of the finite Gaussian translation algebra.

---

## 16.5 Irreducibility Inside Each Parity Sector

### Corollary 16.2

The restrictions of \(\mathfrak G_N\) to \(\mathcal H_+\) and \(\mathcal H_-\) are irreducible.

Equivalently, there is no nontrivial subspace

\[
0\subsetneq W\subsetneq \mathcal H_+
\]

invariant under every Gaussian translation operator, and likewise no such nontrivial invariant subspace inside \(\mathcal H_-\).

### Proof

The restriction of \(\mathfrak G_N\) to \(\mathcal H_+\) is the full matrix algebra

\[
M_{\lceil N/2\rceil}(\mathbb C),
\]

and the restriction to \(\mathcal H_-\) is

\[
M_{\lfloor N/2\rfloor}(\mathbb C).
\]

A full matrix algebra acts irreducibly on its defining vector space. Therefore each parity sector is irreducible. \(\square\)

This gives a precise meaning to the statement that reflection is the only universal exact symmetry: after the parity split, no further common invariant decomposition remains.

---

## 16.6 Dimension Formula

### Corollary 16.3

The complex dimension of the finite Gaussian translation algebra is

\[
\boxed{
\dim_{\mathbb C}\mathfrak G_N
=
\left\lceil\frac N2\right\rceil^2
+
\left\lfloor\frac N2\right\rfloor^2.
}
\]

Hence

\[
\dim_{\mathbb C}\mathfrak G_N
=
\begin{cases}
N^2/2,&N\text{ even},\\[4pt]
(N^2+1)/2,&N\text{ odd}.
\end{cases}
\]

This is the maximal possible dimension for a matrix algebra subject only to reflection symmetry.

## 16.7 A Basis from Arbitrary Distinct Nonnegative Shifts

The earlier sampling statement can be sharpened from genericity to universality.

### Proposition 16.4 (Chebyshev-Vandermonde Sampling)

Let

\[
0\le s_1<\cdots<s_N.
\]

Then

\[
\boxed{
\{T_{s_1},\ldots,T_{s_N}\}
}
\]

is a basis of \(\mathcal T_N^{\mathrm{sym}}\).

In particular, any \(N\) distinct nonnegative shifts span the full symmetric Toeplitz operator system.

### Proof

Write

\[
T_s
=
\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}a_k(s)H_k,
\]

where \(H_k\) is the symmetric Toeplitz basis matrix supported on the diagonals \(|i-j|=k\). For the paired Gaussian translation kernel,

\[
a_k(s)
=
2e^{-s^2/(4\sigma^2)}e^{-k^2h^2/(4\sigma^2)}
\cosh(k\alpha s),
\]

with

\[
\alpha=\frac{h}{2\sigma^2}>0.
\]

The row factors

\[
2e^{-k^2h^2/(4\sigma^2)}
\]

and the column factors

\[
e^{-s_j^2/(4\sigma^2)}
\]

are nonzero, so invertibility of the coefficient matrix is equivalent to invertibility of

\[
\bigl[\cosh(k\alpha s_j)\bigr]_{
0\le k\le N-1,\,1\le j\le N}.
\]

Set

\[
u_j=\cosh(\alpha s_j).
\]

Using the Chebyshev identity

\[
\cosh(kx)=T_k(\cosh x),
\]

we obtain the matrix

\[
[T_k(u_j)].
\]

The polynomials \(T_0,\ldots,T_{N-1}\) have degrees \(0,\ldots,N-1\) and leading coefficients

\[
1,1,2,4,\ldots,2^{N-2}.
\]

Therefore

\[
\det[T_k(u_j)]
=
2^{(N-1)(N-2)/2}
\prod_{1\le i<j\le N}(u_j-u_i),
\]

up to the sign determined by the ordering convention.

Because \(\cosh(\alpha s)\) is strictly increasing on \([0,\infty)\), the nodes \(u_j\) are distinct. The determinant is therefore nonzero, so the matrices \(T_{s_1},\ldots,T_{s_N}\) are linearly independent. Since \(\dim \mathcal T_N^{\mathrm{sym}}=N\), they form a basis. \(\square\)

## 16.8 Arithmetic Shifts at the Level of Linear Span

Let

\[
0\le s_1<\cdots<s_N
\]

be any distinct prime-power logarithms, for example the first \(N\) distinct values among

\[
\log p^m.
\]

By Proposition 16.4,

\[
\{T_{s_1},\ldots,T_{s_N}\}
\]

is automatically a basis of \(\mathcal T_N^{\mathrm{sym}}\).

Thus arithmetic shifts are not distinguished at the level of linear span. Any distinction between arithmetic and non-arithmetic shifts must arise from smaller generating sets, multiplicative structure, commutators, or the departure from a uniform grid.

---

## 16.9 What the Uniform Theory Determines

The uniform continuous model is now completely described by the following chain:

\[
\operatorname{span}\{T_s:s\in I\}
=
\mathcal T_N^{\mathrm{sym}},
\]

\[
(\mathcal T_N^{\mathrm{sym}})'
=
\operatorname{span}\{I,J\},
\]

\[
\operatorname{alg}^{*}\{T_s:s\in I\}
=
\{J\}',
\]

\[
\{J\}'
\cong
M_{\lceil N/2\rceil}(\mathbb C)
\oplus
M_{\lfloor N/2\rfloor}(\mathbb C).
\]

No numerical input is required anywhere in this chain.

---

## 16.10 Remaining Problems

The structure theorem closes the continuous equally spaced model, but it isolates two genuinely different open directions.

### 16.10.1 Two-shift generation

For distinct shifts \(s\) and \(t\), determine when

\[
\boxed{
\{T_s,T_t\}'
=
\operatorname{span}\{I,J\}.
}
\]

If this equality holds, then

\[
\operatorname{alg}^{*}(T_s,T_t)
=
\{J\}'.
\]

This question cannot be settled by linear span, since two matrices span at most a two-dimensional subspace of \(\mathcal T_N^{\mathrm{sym}}\). Any proof must use products and finite-boundary noncommutativity.

### 16.10.2 Non-uniform symmetric grids

On a non-uniform symmetric packet grid, the kernels are no longer Toeplitz. The span theorem and sine-basis commutant proof therefore do not transfer directly.

The corresponding problem is to determine whether reflection remains the complete exact symmetry:

\[
\boxed{
\{T_s:s\in I\}'
\stackrel{?}{=}
\operatorname{span}\{I,J\}.
}
\]

This includes the arithmetic section grids used elsewhere in the finite Weil laboratory.

---

## 16.11 Summary

The finite Gaussian translation algebra on an equally spaced symmetric grid is not merely constrained by parity. It is exactly the full algebra of parity-preserving operators:

\[
\boxed{
\mathfrak G_N
=
\{J\}'
\cong
M_{\lceil N/2\rceil}(\mathbb C)
\oplus
M_{\lfloor N/2\rfloor}(\mathbb C).
}
\]

Its center is

\[
\boxed{
Z(\mathfrak G_N)
=
\operatorname{span}\{I,J\},
}
\]

and its two parity sectors are irreducible.

Together with the Chebyshev-Vandermonde sampling theorem, this completes the algebraic classification of the continuous uniform-grid Gaussian translation model.
